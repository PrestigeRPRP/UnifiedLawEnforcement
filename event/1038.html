Traffic Stop

Lights, Siren, Action!

About Traffic Stop and Risk

This document details the protocols for conducting both low-risk and high-risk traffic stops, ensuring adherence to the San Andreas Penal Code (SAPC). It prioritizes officer safety, legal justification, and clear communication throughout the stop process.

Prior to Initiating a Traffic Stop:

Reasonable Doubt:
An officer must establish, beyond a reasonable doubt, that a violation of the San Andreas Penal Code (SAPC) has occurred.


Valid Violations:
A stop should only be initiated for a violation explicitly listed in the SAPC or for a BOLOed vehicle.


Witness Requirement:
The officer must have personally witnessed the infraction.


Initiating the Stop:

    Radio Call: The officer should announce the stop over the radio, providing details:
      • Call Sign
      • Vehicle Description
      • Type of Vehicle / Exact Model if Known
      • Number of Occupants
      • Location
      • Backup

    Example: This is 311 (ADAM-LINCOLN-SQUAD) to dispatch, I have a 10-38 of a Grey, 4 Door, Sedan. with 2 Occupants here at San Andreas Avenue. I need 10-78

    Safe Approach: The officer should approach the vehicle from the safest side, avoiding the "policeman's alley."
    Identification: The officer should identify themselves to the driver.
    Notification of Violation: The officer should inform the driver of the specific SAPC violation observed.
    Citation or Warning: The officer should decide whether to issue a citation or a warning, ensuring that the citation is for a valid SAPC violation.

High-Risk Traffic Stop



A high-risk traffic stop is a potentially dangerous situation where there's an increased threat to public safety or officer safety. This could be due to factors such as the driver having an active felony warrant, being a suspect in a serious crime, or exhibiting erratic or threatening behavior.

Initiating a High-Risk Stop:


    Request Backup: The initiating officer should immediately request at least one backup unit and wait for their arrival before exiting the patrol vehicle.
    Vehicle Positioning: Both the primary and secondary units should position their vehicles in a tactical manner, such as offsetting each other and leaving doors open for cover.
    Driver Commands: The primary officer should give clear and concise commands to the driver:

    • Turn off the engine.
    • Throw the keys outside the vehicle.
    • Open the door using the exterior handle.
    • Exit the vehicle slowly, facing away from the officers.

    Controlled Movement: The primary officer should guide the suspect to walk backward toward their voice while the secondary units cover the vehicle.
    Arrest and Search: Once the suspect is handcuffed, the primary officer should search them and secure them in the patrol vehicle. Secondary units should ensure all occupants are out of the vehicle and that it's secure.
