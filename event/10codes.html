ABOUT CODE and Signals

Signal 100

While on patrol, you might hear a unit call out "10-80" over the radio, followed by a series of beeps. Dispatch will then announce that "Signal 100 is now in effect."

Signal 100 is activated during critical incidents, such as pursuits or active shooter situations, to ensure open radio channels for urgent communications. Units can then transmit vital information, like locations or suspect descriptions, without interference.

10 codes

Are a standardized system of communication used by law enforcement agencies to quickly and efficiently convey information over the radio. This system helps to maintain clarity, brevity, and security in police communications.

Code Responses

Code One (1): Officers must adhere to all traffic laws and respond without activating lights or sirens. This code is appropriate for situations where there is no immediate threat or emergency, such as when a citizen wishes to file a police report.

Code Two (2): Officers should follow traffic laws but may activate their siren and lights cautiously, especially at intersections or when encountering obstacles that could hinder their response time. This code is suitable for incidents that require a prompt response but do not necessitate a full-fledged emergency.

Code Three (3): Officers will respond with lights and sirens the whole way to the scene. Officers will be careful when clearing intersections and drive responsibly. Remember an Officer's response is needed if they are called to respond to Code Three (3). Officers need to make sure they get to the scene safe. Consult the *TBA* for any questions or clarification.
